POTATO MAZE

CSS-385 Unity Game Project

DESCRIPTION

This game was inspired by the classic Flash game World's Hardest Game. That game was deceptively simple—which was aimed to mimic. You simply navigate the square character to the endzone and collect all the coins without touching the circles. However, the game’s proportions made each level difficult due to speed, size, and direction of the obstacles. We aimed to copy this idea but add another challenge: movement manipulation.

Our game requires the player to move the player sprite, a potato in the shape of a circle, through the level and grab all the collectibles and reach the flag without hitting any of the saws, spikes or other obstacles. The twist in our game is that each collectible slows the player down by a percentage amount. This means that players must not only dodge the obstacles skillfully, they must also decide which order to attain the collectibles within the level. For example, if there are two collectibles, one in the middle of multiple obstacles and another in the open, we want the player to realize that the open one is a free collectible. It is meant to be collected after the collectible in the middle. This is because the collectible in the middle can only be collected if the player is moving fast. If they collect the free collectible, the player may be too slow to navigate through the obstacles and collect that second collectible.

By employing this movement manipulation, we were able to challenge the player in multiple categories: skill, patience, persistence, and strategy. Skill of the game is required to beat all the levels—there is no avoiding that. Patience is tested on levels that have small gaps of escape and the player must wait to have those gaps open for them. Persistence is also tested in our game as users will fail on levels multiple times and will keep trying. Strategy is tested via the speed manipulation of the collectibles, as previously mentioned, and its impact on the players route through the levels.

We aimed at creating a simple yet addictive game that players will try to beat in one sitting. This is achieved by orienting the game as a simple game that looks easy. Once the player starts playing the game and failing the levels, they motivate themselves that they ‘barely failed’. These small excuses keep the player challenged and now they feel the need to beat that level. Once they beat the level they were stuck on, the new level provides a surprise that jogs their curiosity—and they want to beat this level too. This cycle repeats per level until they have eventually beaten all levels.

We did not gear this game to be a specimen that the player returns to after completing all levels—as there is going to be no change in the content when they come back. However, to accomplish that, we can add leaderboards per level that show the minimum deaths and time of all tries that occurred on that level. This can be a source of entertainment among the players and result in all of them coming back to beat each other’s scores.

In conclusion, we had intended on creating a game based on the classic flash game World's Hardest Game with a twist in speed manipulation. This game is intended to be deceptively simple, and addictive and we believe we managed to achieve those goals. We can further enhance our game with leaderboards for least deaths and time per level to drive a competitive aspect in our game.

PLAY TESTING REPORT

Alpha Playtest

What we had:
  • Playable game tutorial level
  • Internet sprites for our characters
  • Collision detection and level clear
  • Tile Map
What we found:
  • People liked the idea
  • Left the game too quickly
    • Not much user addiction
  • Tile map confused users
What testers recommended:
  • Collisions were aggressive
  • Game was easy
  • Sprites didn't really fit theme
  • Potato slowed down too much (made level boring)

Beta Playtest

What we had:
  • 5 playable levels
  • Corrupted code (waypoints would cause enemies to arc)
  • Level clearing sequence
  • Flags configured so it would spawn only upon collecting all collectibles
What we found:
  • People played the game much longer
  • Toggling visibility of flag would surprise users
  • Although bugged, many players would keep trying to beat the levels
  • No one passed the game in one sitting
What testers recommended:
  • Collisions were aggressive
  • Game was deceptively simple
  • They could see how to pass every level but didn't have the skill to do so

Final Playtest

What we had:
  • 9 playable levels
  • Corrected waypoints
  • Proper level sequence based on difficulty
  • Adjacent levels would be different from one another to keep player engaged
What we found:
  • A lot of players were surprised by certain levels
  • Players spent around 20-30 minutes on our game
  • More emotions were displayed
    • Frustration
    • Excitement
    • Relief
  • One person beat the entire game
  • We had to verbally give tips on certain levels
What testers recommended:
  • One level was unnecessarily slow
  • The difficulty ramps up perfectly
  • They liked the surprise, challenge, and simplicity of the game

Final presentation

What we had:
  • Finished version of the Potato Maze Game
  • Presented our game in front of class
  • Displayed a walkthrough of our game
What we found:
  • Many people wanted to play our game for the final time
  • A lot of addiction to the game
  • Many people forgot how to beat the game via our walkthrough
  • Another person managed to clear the entire game
What testers recommended:
  • Loved the game
  • Simple design and controls juxtapose the difficulty of the game
